Comparative User Evaluation of Training Modalities

A rigorous HCI user study comparing VR immersive training, in-person coaching, and instructional video across task completion rate, learning retention, and user satisfaction — analysed with ANOVA and Chi-squared statistical tests.

2024 Research Project ~6 weeks HCI · Statistics · User Study
Demo Source

Problem

Organisations investing in training programmes lack empirical data comparing the effectiveness of emerging VR-based training against traditional modalities. This study provides controlled experimental evidence to guide training technology investment decisions, using rigorous HCI methodology.

Study Design

1

Participants

24 participants (8 per condition) recruited from university pool, balanced for prior VR experience and domain knowledge. Between-subjects design to avoid learning transfer across conditions.

2

Conditions

Three training modalities: (A) VR immersive simulation, (B) live coaching with an instructor, (C) instructional video. All covered identical content for a standardised assembly task.

3

Measures

Primary: task completion rate and time-on-task. Secondary: retention quiz score at 1-week follow-up. Subjective: NASA-TLX for workload, SUS for usability satisfaction.

4

ANOVA Analysis

One-way ANOVA conducted on task completion time across three groups. Post-hoc Tukey HSD identified pairwise differences. Levene's test confirmed homogeneity of variance.

5

Chi-squared Tests

Chi-squared test of independence applied to completion rate (pass/fail) across conditions. Effect size computed via Cramér's V.

Key Learnings

VR produced the highest 1-week retention scores but also the highest NASA-TLX workload. Coaching achieved the fastest task completion. Video training showed the lowest SUS satisfaction scores.

Between-subjects designs require larger samples to achieve equivalent power versus within-subjects — a limitation acknowledged in future work.

Effect sizes (Cohen's d, Cramér's V) are more informative than p-values alone for practical decision-making.
